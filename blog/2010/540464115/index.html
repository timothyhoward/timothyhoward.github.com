The last days are marked by hallucinations: bears, wolves and aliens prowl the roadside; asphalt cracks rearrange themselves into coded messages. Occasionally, Robic leaps from his bike to square off with shadowy figures that turn out to be mailboxes. In a 2004 race, he turned to see himself pursued by a howling band of black-bearded men on horseback.

From an NY Times feature on what happens to Jure Robic’s mind during extreme endurance events.

When I was doing a lot of running and swimming, at the end of a long run I would get a mild case of these kind of things. I’d mistake inanimate things for animals or people: a shrub could be a dog, a tree trunk could be a gardener but I’d immediately snap out of it. It was that or a state of semi detachment which was really quite a nice feeling, snapping out of that would feel like waking up.

I can’t imagine what it must feel like going through the sleep deprived hell of something like Le Tour Direct.Robic is a very hard man. Via Kottke.
